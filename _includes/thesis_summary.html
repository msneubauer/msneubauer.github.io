The Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO) is a water Cherenkov detector designed to study solar neutrinos. Using one kiloton of heavy water as the target and detection medium, SNO is able to separately measure the flux and the flux of all active neutrinos (≡\equiv , , ) from the Sun. These fluxes are measured directly from the rate of CC ($\nue + d \rightarrow p + p + e^-$) and NC ($\nux + d \rightarrow n + p + \nux$) reactions. They can also be determined by comparing the CC rate with the rate of ES ($\nux + e^- \rightarrow \nux + e^-$) events. Since only electron neutrinos are generated in solar fusion reactions, one can use SNO to look for flavor change as an explanation of the solar deficit observed by other experiments (the “Solar Neutrino Problem”).

SNO began collecting data in November 1999. My thesis entitled Evidence for Electron Neutrino Flavor Change Through Measurement of the Solar Neutrino Flux at SNO presents an analysis of the neutrino data set over a livetime of 169.3 days. I measured the flux from the CC reaction to be 1.78−0.14+0.13×1061.78^{+0.13}_{-0.14} \times 10^6 cm−2^{-2} s−1^{-1}, which is 35% of the flux expected from solar model calculations by Bahcall, et al (BP00 Standard Solar Model). This confirms the solar neutrino deficit, and, for the first time, by measuring the exclusive flux of neutrinos. I have also measured the flux from the ES rate to be 2.56−0.45+0.48×1062.56^{+0.48}_{-0.45} \times 10^6cm−2^{-2} s−1^{-1} or 50% of the expected flux under the assumption of no flavor change. This is consistent with solar neutrino results from the light water experiments. It is also higher than the flux obtained from the CC rate by 2.0σ\sigma. While a ES rate higher than CC is what one would expect for →\rightarrowflavor transformations, the significance of the SNO-only ES-CC difference is quite low, with the error on the difference dominated by low ES statistics.

The Super-Kamiokande experiment had ten times the ES statistics compared to SNO at the time I finished my analysis, with a published neutrino flux after 1258 days of 2.32−0.08+0.09×1062.32^{+0.09}_{-0.08} \times 10^6cm−2^{-2} s−1^{-1}. The flux from my measurement of the SNO CC rate is smaller than the flux inferred from Super-Kamiokande’s ES results by 3.5σ\sigma. Since the sensitivity of the ES rate to and is known (it is about six times smaller than ), one can calculate the flux of non-$\nue$ active neutrinos (ϕνμ,τ\phi_{\nu_{\mu,\tau}}) in . In my thesis, this is measured for the first time to be

ϕνμ,τ=3.62−1.08+1.06×106\phi_{\nu_{\mu,\tau}} = 3.62^{+1.06}_{-1.08} \times 10^6cm−2^{-2} s−1^{-1}

providing evidence for →\rightarrowflavor transformations at the 99.98% confidence level. In addition, the total neutrino flux (ϕtotal\phi_{total}) is measured for the first time to be

ϕνtotal=5.39−1.09+1.07×106\phi_{\nu_{total}} = 5.39^{+1.07}_{-1.09} \times 10^6cm−2^{-2} s−1^{-1}

in agreement with BP00 solar model calculations and confirmed by recent NC rate measurements by SNO. As a result, we can rule out exclusive oscillation of into a sterile neutrino as an explanation of the Solar Neutrino Problem. Instead, we now know that  2/3 of all electron neutrinos change flavor into and/or before reaching the Earth and that the total active neutrino flux agrees with Standard Solar Model calculations (see Figures 9.1 and 9.2 of the thesis). The Sun is now a calibrated neutrino source!

Although evidence for neutrino flavor change from CC and ES rate measurements is the primary result, my thesis also includes other analyses of importance for SNO. I performed the first energy spectrum analysis from the observed CC (number of hit PMTs) distribution in SNO. I found the extracted CC signal above 65 ($$7 MeV of e−e^- energy) to be consistent with an undistorted energy spectrum (some oscillation scenarios predict distortions). I also performed SNO’s first hep neutrino search analysis. The hep neutrinos result from a rare reaction in the solar pp chain and extend to higher energies than neutrinos from . The fact that they have yet to be observed makes them interesting to look for in the SNO energy spectrum. Techniques developed in my thesis currently form the basis for hep neutrino analysis in SNO.

The largest systematic error on CC and ES rate measurements above my chosen energy threshold is determination of the absolute energy scale because this directly affects the number of events one expects to observe in the data above threshold for a given solar neutrino flux. SNO’s energy response is determined by modeling the detector geometry and Cherenkov light generation with input from calibration of detector optical properties. The energy response model is tested with radioactive calibration sources. Since these sources can only sample a finite set of locations in the detector, a typical neutrino analysis will rely heavily upon the accuracy of the model. Optical calibrations were not completed when I was analyzing the neutrino data set, so I developed a method of interpolating the energy scale between calibration data points which greatly reduces sensitivity to optical uncertainties. Testing against other calibration data, I was able to demonstrate that this scheme provides an accurate representation of the detector energy response. The robustness of this approach against a variety of detector effects (e.g. rate effects, time stability) and optical uncertainties proved crucial to the success of my analysis.

Estimating the location and direction of energetic charged particles in the detector is a critical part of solar neutrino analysis in SNO. Many backgrounds originate (by design) in the outer regions of the detector and the only means of rejection is to locate them outside of the target volume or demonstrate their inconsistency with Cherenkov light. In an effort to use as much event information as possible in vertex reconstruction, I developed a new maximum likelihood fitter using PMT time and angular information to find the most likely event vertex/direction and return figures-of-merit for this determination under the fitting hypothesis. Based upon performance, this fitter was chosen by the collaboration as the primary event reconstruction method for analysis during the phase of operation.

In the context of background studies, I developed the method adopted by SNO for estimating external high energy γ\gamma-ray backgrounds. These backgrounds are important because they have energies extending into the solar neutrino energy range and therefore cannot be removed by a simple cut on energy. A far larger solar neutrino background is events initiated by the detector instrumentation itself. In particular, spontaneous emission of light from the PMTs (“PMT flashers”) is the dominant solar neutrino background at high energy. As a result, much analysis effort has gone into learning how to remove these events. I developed two cuts which take advantage of the short time duration of Cherenkov light initiated by neutrino interactions in SNO compared to flashers and other instrumental backgrounds. These cuts which are described in my thesis are used in first-pass filtering of the SNO production data stream.

My work on SNO’s trigger system (for which I was primarily responsible for design and commissioning of the digital system) naturally led to consideration of trigger efficiency. Loosely speaking, the trigger efficiency is the probability of triggering SNO in response to time-correlated PMT hits from Cherenkov light. In addition to being a crucial input to physics analyses, the trigger efficiency also has a profound impact on how the data is collected. The ability to collect quality neutrino data at as low an energy threshold as possible depends critically on being both very efficient at triggering on events above hardware threshold and very inefficient at triggering on events below threshold where radioactive backgrounds become exponentially large. The combination of a highly efficient trigger ($3.5turn−onfrom103.5 turn-on from 10% to 90%) and very low backgrounds currently allows SNO to run with a hardware trigger threshold of 16 (<$2 MeV) - a level never before achieved by water a Cherenkov experiment. I developed the methods for determining trigger efficiency and performed SNO’s official trigger efficiency measurement.

In summary, my thesis is a demonstration solar neutrino flavor change as an explanation of one of the oldest puzzles in modern experimental physics - Solar Neutrino Problem. While the implications for particle physics are still being understood, these results provide strong evidence for physics beyond the Standard Model in the form of neutrino mass and mixing.
